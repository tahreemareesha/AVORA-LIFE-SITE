Struggling to Fall Asleep Faster? 10 Proven Tips to Help You Overcome It

Poor sleep shows up in memory, mood, and metabolism. Here are ten natural, research-backed techniques to fix your sleep patterns.

Dec 4, 2025 8 min read

Tossing and turning but can't sleep? Memory problems, mood swings, weaker metabolism, and poor concentration are common results of sleep deprivation. Here are ten natural, effective, science-backed ways to improve your sleep.

1. A consistent sleep schedule

Going to bed and waking up at the same time every day supports your circadian rhythm and helps you fall asleep faster. Research from Harvard Health has linked a regular sleep schedule to falling asleep 20–30% faster.

2. The military sleep method

Originally used by soldiers to fall asleep in high-stress conditions. The steps: relax your facial muscles, drop your shoulders and let your arms hang loose, exhale slowly, clear your mind, and visualize a calm scene. The method traces back to a 1981 book, Relax and Win: Champion Performance, and reportedly works within about two minutes.

3. The 4-7-8 breathing technique

Inhale for 4 seconds, hold for 7, exhale for 8. This slows your heart rate and calms your nervous system. A study in the Journal of Clinical Psychology (2015) found deep breathing practices reduce anxiety and improve sleep quality.

4. Reverse psychology: try to stay awake

Known as paradoxical intention, this technique removes the pressure of "trying" to sleep, which itself relaxes the brain. Studies on people with insomnia found this approach led to falling asleep faster with less performance anxiety around sleep.

5. Wear socks to bed

It sounds unusual, but a 2018 study found participants who wore socks to bed fell asleep about 7.5 minutes faster and slept 32 minutes longer. The mechanism is vasodilation — warm feet trigger heat loss from the body's core, signaling the brain it's time to sleep.

6. Read something boring

Neutral, unexciting reading material — old textbooks or manuals rather than thrillers — slows the brain down naturally and eases the transition to sleep.

7. Digital sunset

Shutting off screens 1–2 hours before bed prevents blue light from suppressing melatonin production. A 2014 study published in PNAS found that people reading on light-emitting devices took longer to fall asleep, got less REM sleep, and were less alert the next morning compared to those reading printed books.

8. Gentle yoga or stretching before bed

Just 5–10 minutes of light yoga or stretching — child's pose, legs-up-the-wall — helps calm the body and brain. Research published in the Journal of Clinical Psychology found yoga improves sleep quality and reduces insomnia symptoms by lowering stress.

9. Create a bedtime ritual

A simple, repeated, calming sequence — skincare, a few pages of a book, light stretching — trains your brain to associate the routine with sleep. Consistency matters more than the specific activity.

10. Progressive muscle relaxation (PMR)

Starting at your toes, tense each muscle group for about five seconds, then release, working upward through calves, thighs, arms, shoulders, and face. As you move up, the body progressively relaxes and prepares for sleep.

These aren't just tips — each is grounded in published research on sleep physiology and behavior. Try one at a time rather than all ten at once, and give each a few nights before judging it.

Final thoughts

None of these techniques work identically for everyone. Start with whichever feels most doable tonight, and build from there.
